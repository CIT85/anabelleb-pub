Getting Started!

Tools

  1. Container/Planters: Pots with drainage holes! Proper drainage is crucial to prevent overwatering adn root rot.
  2. Quality Potting Soil: Use a good quality, well-draining potting mix designed for indoor plants. Avoid garden soil, it's too heeavy and can compact containers.
  3. Watering Can: A small watering can with a lon gpsout allows for precise watering. Choose one with gentle flow to avoid disturbing the soil.
  4. Humidity Tray: Many herbs, such as basil, thrive more in a more humid environment. A humidity try can increase the moisture around the plants.
  5. Pruning Shears: A pair of clean sharp scissors or pruning shears are needed to maintain plants and snip herbs for cooking.
  6. Plant Markers: These help keep your plants labelled so there is no mistaking your basil for mint.

Soil Types

Which Soil Should You Choose?

  • For Easy, General Use: Go for general potting soil if you're growing a variety of herbs and want something versatile and easy.
  • For Excellent Drainage: If you're growing herbs that prefer drier conditions, like rosemary or thyme, cactus or succulent soil is a great choice.
  • For Consistent Moisture: For herbs that need regular moisture but not soggy conditions (like basil and parsley), consider coconut coir mixed with perlite.
  • For Specialized Herbs: If you're focusing on a specific herb type or growing in a hydroponic system, herb-specific potting mix or soil-less mixes may be more beneficial.

Choosing Your Plants!

These are some of the most commonly used and beginner friendly herbs to start your indoor garden.

Herbs to start your indoor gargen.

Basil
Basil is a popular herb often used in Italian, Mediterranean, and Southeast Asian cuisines. Common Uses: Pesto, Italian dishes (like pizza and pasta), Thai cuisine, and as a garnish.
Mint
Mint is a fast-growing herb that's commonly known for its strong refreshing flavor. Common Uses: Teas, cocktails (like mojitos), desserts, and salads.
Parsley
It's often used fresh as a garnish but also has a mildly bitter, earthy flavor that complements many dishes. Common Uses: Garnish, soups, stews, salads, and as a flavoring in sauces.
Chives
Chives are a member of the onion family and have a mild, onion-like flavor. Their slender, green stems are often used as a garnish or in salads and other dishes. Common Uses: Salads, potato dishes, dips, and as a garnish for soups.
Thyme
Thyme is a woody herb with a strong, savory flavor commonly used in Mediterranean, French, and Middle Eastern cooking. It’s especially good for slow-cooked dishes. Common Uses: Roasted meats, soups, stews, and as a seasoning for vegetables.
Cilantro
Cilantro is an herb with bright, fresh leaves and a unique, somewhat spicy flavor. It’s widely used in Latin American, Indian, and Asian cuisines. Common Uses: Salsas, curries, salads, and as a garnish in many dishes.

Plant Maintenance & Care Routines

Care Rountines!

Plant Watering Schedule Sunlight Needs Repotting
Basil Water every 2-3 days, keep soil consistently moist. Avoid waterlogging. Full sun (6+ hours/day) Repot every 1 year, or when plant becomes root-bound.
Mint Water 1-2 times a week, keep soil moist but not soggy. Indirect sunlight, can tolerate partial shade. Repot every 1-2 years. Mint is invasive, so it’s often better to keep it in a pot.
Parsley Water 2-3 times a week, allowing soil to dry out slightly between waterings. Full sun to partial shade (4-6 hours/day) Repot every 1 year. Parsley tends to be short-lived, so frequent replacement is common.
Chives Water once a week or when the top inch of soil is dry. Full sun to partial shade (4-6 hours/day) Repot every 1-2 years when the plant becomes dense.
Thyme Water once a week, allowing the soil to dry out completely between waterings. Full sun (6-8 hours/day) Repot every 2 years or when it becomes too woody.
Cilantro Water 2-3 times a week, keeping soil moist but well-drained. Full sun (6 hours/day) or partial shade Repot every 1 year or when it becomes too tall or leggy.

Pruning and Harvesting

How to Prune and Harvest Your Herbs!

Regular pruning helps your herbs grow bushier and encourages new growth. For most herbs, it's best to harvest leaves by pinching off the tips rather than cutting them with scissors to avoid damaging the plant.

To prune herbs, simply use clean, sharp scissors to snip off the top few inches above a leaf node, remove any dead or yellowing leaves, and trim regularly to encourage bushier growth.

When harvesting, always take no more than one-third of the plant at a time to ensure it continues to grow healthily.
